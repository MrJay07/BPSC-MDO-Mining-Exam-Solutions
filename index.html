1.1. Drillability of Rocks

Drillability refers to the inherent resistance a rock mass presents to drilling penetration. It serves as a crucial indicator of drilling efficiency, footage rates, and the overall difficulty associated with creating blastholes. This property is often quantified by the rate at which a drill bit penetrates the rock (e.g., centimeters per minute) or through a calculated drillability factor, which is typically derived from empirical studies conducted both in the field and in laboratory settings.¹

Several parameters significantly influence rock drillability:

  • Hardness: This is a fundamental mechanical property, commonly assessed using scales such as the Mohs hardness scale. Minerals with higher Mohs numbers are inherently harder and thus offer greater resistance to drilling. Since most rocks are composed of multiple minerals, it is essential to conduct several tests across a rock sample to determine its average hardness for a more accurate drillability assessment.
  • Texture and Density: The internal structure of a rock profoundly impacts its drillability. Fine-grained rocks, characterized by tightly packed and cohesive mineral grains, generally exhibit higher resistance to drilling, leading to slower penetration rates. In contrast, coarse-grained rocks may display variable drillability, which is largely dependent on the strength of the bonding between individual grains. The specific orientation and interlocking patterns of these grains further influence the efficiency of energy transfer during the drilling process. Porous rocks, containing numerous void spaces, typically require less mechanical energy to fracture due to their reduced overall cohesion. Conversely, dense rocks with low porosity demand higher drilling forces due to stronger intergranular bonds.
  • Rock Fracture Pattern: The presence, arrangement, orientation, spacing, and continuity of natural fractures or joints within a rock mass are critical determinants of how easily it can be drilled. Rocks with closely spaced fractures or joints generally exhibit higher drillability because these discontinuities act as inherent planes of weakness, facilitating the breaking and removal of material by drilling tools. The orientation (dip angle) of these fractures relative to the drilling direction also plays a significant role in dictating crack propagation paths and overall drilling efficiency.¹
  • Formation/Rock Mass Structure: This parameter describes the macroscopic structural condition of the entire rock mass. High drilling rates are typically achievable in massive, solid, or dense rock formations with minimal seams. Conversely, blocky and seamy rock masses, characterized by numerous discontinuities and fragmented structures, generally lead to slower drilling operations.

Quantification methods for rock drillability include:

  • Sievers' J-miniature drill test: This test measures the surface hardness of a rock by determining the depth of a small hole drilled after a specific number of revolutions.¹
  • Brittleness test ($S_{20}$): This method quantifies a rock's resistance to impact by measuring the percentage of material that passes through a standard mesh after a series of hammer drops.
  • Drilling Rate Index (DRI): Developed in Norway, the DRI integrates the results from two distinct laboratory tests to provide a standardized metric for comparing the drillability of various rock types.¹

It is important to recognize that these rock properties do not act in isolation. The actual drilling efficiency is a product of their complex interrelationship. For instance, a rock with high intrinsic hardness might be expected to have low drillability. However, if this hard rock is extensively fractured with closely spaced and favorably oriented joints, these planes of weakness can significantly reduce the effective resistance to drilling. Conversely, a softer, more porous rock, if massive and unfractured, might still present considerable drilling challenges due to its cohesive structure. This understanding underscores that optimizing drilling operations necessitates a comprehensive geological and geotechnical assessment that goes beyond merely characterizing individual rock properties. It requires a holistic view of the rock mass behavior to accurately predict and improve drilling performance.

1.2. Slake Durability

►

Slake durability is a geotechnical property that quantifies a rock sample's resistance to weakening and disintegration when subjected to repeated cycles of drying and wetting. This test is particularly relevant for assessing the long-term stability of rock slopes, excavations, and aggregates exposed to environmental weathering processes.

The slake durability test procedure involves the following key steps and apparatus:

  • Apparatus: The primary apparatus consists of a test drum, which is a 2.00 mm standard mesh cylinder (100 mm unobstructed length, 140 mm diameter) with a solid fixed base and a removable lid. This drum must be able to withstand temperatures up to $105^{\circ}C$ and maintain its shape during use. The drum is supported horizontally within a trough, which can be filled with a slaking fluid (typically tap water at $20^{\circ}C$) to a level 20 mm below the drum axis, allowing free rotation. A motor rotates the drum, and a balance capable of weighing the drum plus sample to an accuracy of 0.5 g is required.³
  • Procedure:
    1. A representative sample, typically comprising ten rock lumps, each weighing between 40-60 g (total sample mass 450-550 g), is selected. The maximum grain size should not exceed 3 mm, and lumps should be roughly spherical with rounded corners.³
    2. The sample is placed in a clean drum and dried to a constant mass in an oven at $105^{\circ}C$, usually taking 2 to 6 hours. The mass of the drum plus the dry sample (Mass A) is recorded after cooling.³
    3. The drum, with the sample inside and lid replaced, is mounted in the trough and coupled to the motor. The trough is filled with water to the specified level.³
    4. The drum is rotated for 200 revolutions over a period of 10 minutes (±0.5 min).³
    5. After the first cycle, the drum is removed, the lid is taken off, and the drum plus the retained rock portion is dried to constant mass at $105^{\circ}C.$ The mass of the drum plus retained portion (Mass B) is recorded after cooling.³
    6. Steps (c) to (e) are repeated for a second cycle, and the mass of the drum plus the retained portion (Mass C) is recorded.³
    7. Finally, the clean, empty drum's mass (Mass D) is recorded.³
  • Calculations: The slake-durability index (second cycle) is calculated as the percentage ratio of the final dry sample mass (after two cycles) to the initial dry sample mass. A higher index indicates a more durable rock type. For example, a rock with a 96.4% slake durability index is classified as highly durable, while very high durability is above 99%, and very low durability is below 60%.³

The slake durability test provides a quantitative measure of a rock's resistance to weathering and disintegration caused by wetting and drying cycles. This is crucial for engineering applications where rock masses are exposed to environmental elements, as it helps predict their long-term performance and stability. For instance, in slope stability analysis, rocks with low slake durability are more prone to breakdown and erosion, which can compromise the integrity of the slope over time.

1.3. Origin and Distribution of Coal Deposits

►

Vast deposits of coal primarily originate from ancient wetlands, often referred to as "coal forests," which covered significant tropical land areas during the late Carboniferous (Pennsylvanian) and Permian geological periods. The formation process, known as coalification, begins when dead plant matter accumulates in these wetlands and is protected from oxidation, typically by being submerged in mud or acidic water. This anaerobic environment prevents complete decomposition, leading to the formation of peat. Over geological time, these peat bogs, which sequester immense quantities of carbon, become deeply buried by subsequent layers of sediment. The increasing pressure and temperature from burial transform the peat through various stages (lignite, sub-bituminous, bituminous, and anthracite) into coal.

While coal deposits are found in most geological periods, approximately 90% of all significant coal beds were deposited during the Carboniferous and Permian periods.⁴ Paradoxically, this era coincided with the Late Paleozoic icehouse, a period characterized by global glaciation. The drop in global sea levels associated with this glaciation exposed vast continental shelves that were previously submerged. Additionally, increased erosion due to the lowered base level led to the formation of wide river deltas. These extensive areas of wetlands provided ideal conditions for the widespread accumulation of plant material and subsequent coal formation.⁴

A significant tectonic factor contributing to the immense coal deposition during the late Carboniferous was the presence of the Central Pangean Mountains. This enormous mountain range, running along the equator, reached its greatest elevation during this period. Climate modeling suggests that these mountains created a region of year-round heavy precipitation, distinct from a typical monsoon climate with dry seasons. Such continuous wet conditions were essential for the preservation of peat in the coal swamps, preventing its decomposition and facilitating the large-scale accumulation necessary for vast coal deposits. Although rare, coal is also known to exist in Precambrian strata, predating land plants, and is presumed to have originated from algal residues. Coal seams are sometimes interbedded with other sediments in cyclothems, which are thought to result from glacial cycles causing fluctuations in sea level, alternately exposing and flooding continental shelf areas.⁴

Globally, approximately 8,000 million tonnes (Mt) of coal are produced annually, with hard coal accounting for about 90% and lignite for 10%. As of 2018, slightly over half of this production came from underground mines, with the industry employing nearly 2.7 million workers worldwide. China is the largest coal producer, mining almost half of the world's coal, followed by India with about a tenth of global production. Indonesia was the largest exporter in 2022, followed by Australia and Russia. Major importers include China, India, and Japan. The price of metallurgical coal, used for coke production, is significantly higher and more volatile than thermal coal due to its lower sulfur requirements and more extensive cleaning processes.⁴

1.4. Contour Line

►

In surveying and mapping, a contour line is an imaginary line on the ground that connects points of equal elevation above or below a reference datum, typically mean sea level. These lines are fundamental for representing the three-dimensional topography of a land surface on a two-dimensional map. A collection of contour lines, known as a contour map or contour survey, provides a visual representation of the vertical and horizontal shape (topography) of an area.

The characteristics and significance of contour lines are as follows:

  • Elevation Representation: Each contour line represents a specific, constant elevation. The difference in elevation between successive contour lines is known as the contour interval, which is typically uniform across a single map (e.g., 0.25m or 10cm for detailed surveys).
  • Slope Indication: The spacing between contour lines directly indicates the steepness of the terrain. When contour lines are closely spaced, it signifies a steep slope. Conversely, widely spaced contour lines indicate a gentle slope or relatively flat terrain.
  • Water Flow Visualization: Contour maps are invaluable for visualizing how water will flow over a property. Water always flows perpendicular to contour lines, from higher elevations to lower ones. This allows for the identification of natural watercourses, gullies, areas prone to water collection, and potential soil erosion zones.
  • Planning and Design: Understanding land contours is essential for various engineering and agricultural applications. For instance, in construction projects, contour mapping is a critical tool for site analysis, calculating earthwork volumes (cut and fill), and verifying that the land's terraforming aligns with the construction plan. For agriculture, particularly tree crops, planting along the contours is often the best strategy to maximize yield and manage water. Roads and tracks are often designed to follow contours to minimize excavation and construction costs.

Traditionally, contour surveys involved manual measurements by survey teams using poles and tripods, a time-consuming process. Modern techniques, such as aerial contour mapping using PPK-equipped drones, can cover large areas rapidly (e.g., up to 300 acres per hour) with high precision (2-3 cm), identifying features that might be overlooked by traditional ground surveys. While drone mapping relies on extrapolating between visible ground points, Lidar mapping can penetrate vegetation to provide more accurate underlying ground levels, especially in areas with thick cover. A registered surveyor is typically required if the data is used for construction, engineering, or land title registration.

1.5. Locked Coil Rope

►

A locked coil rope is a specialized type of wire rope designed for high-strength, low-stretch, and non-rotating applications, particularly prevalent in deep shaft mining. Its unique construction provides superior performance characteristics compared to conventional stranded ropes, making it suitable for demanding environments such as Koepe friction winders and drum winders.⁸

Key features and advantages of locked coil ropes include:

  • Construction: The rope's characteristic design incorporates Z-profile wires in its outer layer. These Z-shaped wires interlock tightly, creating an exceptionally smooth outer surface. This smooth surface minimizes friction and wear on sheave linings (drums and Koepe wheels), contributing to extended service life and reduced maintenance costs. The interlocking nature of the Z-wires also prevents them from unraveling or "unlocking" under tension.
  • High Strength and Low Stretch: Locked coil ropes are engineered for powerful, high-strength applications, making them ideal for lifting heavy loads in deep shafts. Their construction results in low stretch characteristics, which is critical for precise control in winding operations.
  • Non-Rotating Property: Unlike conventional ropes that tend to rotate under load, locked coil ropes are designed to be non-rotating. This stability is crucial for maintaining control and safety in shaft operations, preventing twisting of conveyances or loads.
  • Fatigue Resistance: Precision engineering and the high tensile strength of the interlocking Z-wires contribute to superior fatigue resistance. The use of high ductility wires further minimizes the occurrence of internal wire breaks caused by dynamic stresses, enhancing overall performance and extending the rope's operational life.⁸
  • Compatibility and Customization: The smooth outer surface makes these ropes highly compatible with sheave linings. Furthermore, Z-Lock ropes can be specifically designed and manufactured to be compatible with particular drum grooves, ensuring optimal fit and performance within existing winding systems.⁸
  • Cost-Effectiveness: While initial investment might be higher, the long service life, reduced wear on equipment, and enhanced operational efficiency due to high fatigue performance and minimized internal wire breaks contribute to overall cost-effectiveness over the rope's lifespan.

Locked coil ropes are part of a broader range of mining ropes designed to withstand harsh conditions in both surface and underground mines. Manufacturers emphasize rigorous testing procedures to meet technical specifications for challenging working environments. Innovations like fully plasticated mining ropes, which involve plastic filling in the valleys between strands, offer additional benefits. The plastic cushions the strands, prevents inter-strand contact, reduces wear on sheaves and drums, and locks lubrication within the rope, further improving rope life and operational efficiency.

1.6. Concept of Ore Liberation

►

Ore liberation in mineral processing refers to the process of physically separating valuable ore minerals from the surrounding unwanted gangue minerals.¹⁰ The fundamental principle behind mineral recovery operations, such as ore dressing and concentration, relies on methods that differentiate particles based on their physical or chemical properties. For individual minerals to be effectively separated, each particle must ideally contain only one mineral.¹⁰ If a particle contains two or more minerals, physical separation methods alone cannot achieve complete separation.¹⁰ Therefore, liberation is the process by which individual minerals are physically detached from each other.

The degree of ore liberation is primarily a function of the rock's texture and the differences in size and mechanical properties between the valuable minerals and the gangue minerals.¹¹ During comminution (crushing and grinding), there is a natural tendency towards liberation. Particles smaller than the original mineral grains within the ore can emerge as single-mineral particles when they form entirely within a specific mineral grain.¹⁰ The concept of a "characteristic grain size" is useful here, providing an indication of the size to which the material must be reduced to achieve adequate liberation.¹⁰

Mineral liberation can occur through two primary mechanisms:

  • Liberation by Comminution (Random Breakage): This occurs through a random fracture mechanism where cracks propagate directly through the mineral grains.¹¹ In this type of breakage, particles are reduced to finer sizes with a predictable unimodal distribution, resulting in a relatively constant composition across different size fractions because the ore texture, grain boundaries, or mineral properties do not significantly influence the breakage pattern.¹¹
  • Liberation by Detachment (Non-Random Breakage): This mechanism involves fractures or cracks propagating preferentially along the grain boundaries, leading to the selective liberation of different mineral phases.¹¹ Unlike random breakage, this type of comminution results in variations in mineral composition across different size fractions.¹¹ Non-random breakage can further be categorized into:
    • Interfacial Breakage: Fractures occur specifically between different mineral grains or along phase boundaries, allowing for preferential liberation.¹⁰
    • Preferential Breakage: This happens when the characteristics of the different mineral phases are distinct, causing breakage to occur at varying rates depending on the mineral properties of the ore.¹⁰

Understanding how comminution influences mineral liberation is a critical first step in identifying ores suitable for pre-concentration.¹¹ Early rejection of low-grade materials through selective comminution or grade-by-size separation during coarse comminution can significantly reduce capital expenditure and energy consumption in mining operations, while also simplifying environmental permitting.¹¹ The distribution of mineral grades across different size fractions during coarse comminution processes is often not uniform, as breakage is influenced by mineralogy and texture.¹¹ Therefore, developing test protocols to analyze and characterize grade-by-size variation during compression breakage is essential for optimizing mineral processing strategies.¹¹

1.7. Cutoff Grade

►

The cutoff grade (COG) is a fundamental economic parameter in mining, representing the minimum grade (or value) at which mineralized material can be economically mined or processed.¹² Material with a grade above the cutoff is classified as ore, while material below it is considered waste or gangue.¹³ The selection of the correct cutoff grade is a strategic decision with significant implications for mine design, production planning, cash flow, and the overall profitability and sustainability of a mining operation.¹²

The determination of cutoff grade involves a trade-off between the revenue generated by the mineralized material and the costs associated with its extraction and processing.¹² The basic formula for calculating a break-even cutoff grade is:

$$Cut-off~grade=(Costs-Byproduct~Credits)\div(Commodity~Price\times Recovery).¹⁴$$

Several factors influence the determination and adaptation of cutoff grades:

  • Economic Environment: Cutoff grades are dynamic and constantly adjusted in response to changes in the economic environment, particularly fluctuations in metal prices and mining costs.¹³ When commodity prices rise, lower-grade material becomes economically viable, allowing for a lower cutoff grade. Conversely, when prices fall, only higher-grade material remains profitable, necessitating a higher cutoff grade.¹⁴
  • Mining Costs: The cost of extracting and processing material is a primary driver. For instance, shallow deposits typically have lower mining costs, which allows for lower cutoff grades. Open-pit mining methods, being less expensive per tonne of material moved, enable the profitable extraction of lower-grade material. In contrast, deep deposits or those requiring underground mining methods incur higher expenses, thus demanding higher cutoff grades to offset these increased costs.¹⁴
  • Processing Costs and Unwanted Material: The presence of unwanted material within an ore block can increase processing costs. This factor is also considered when classifying material as ore or waste, as it directly impacts profitability.¹³
  • Strategic Objectives: Cutoff grades are selected to achieve specific objectives, such as maximizing total profits, immediate profits, or present value.¹³ It is a strategic variable that significantly impacts mine design and resource utilization.¹³

The cutoff grade directly influences the size and quality of a mineral resource or reserve estimate.¹² A higher cutoff grade results in a smaller but higher-grade resource, while a lower cutoff grade yields a larger but lower-grade resource.¹⁴ This fundamental decision cascades through the entire resource estimation process and affects exploration strategies and development decisions.¹⁴ Companies often provide grade-tonnage curves to illustrate the sensitivity of the resource to changes in cutoff grade, which is crucial for investors and economic evaluations.¹⁴

1.8. Ultimate Pit Limit

►

The ultimate pit limit (UPL), also known as the final pit limit, defines the maximum boundary of an open-pit mine beyond which further excavation is no longer economically viable.¹⁵ It represents the final configuration of the mine at the end of its operational life, designed to maximize profitability or Net Present Value (NPV) over the project's lifespan.¹⁵

The determination of the ultimate pit limit is a complex optimization problem that considers various technical and economic factors:

  • Economic Viability: A fundamental criterion for including a block of material within the UPL is its ability to cover all associated costs, including mining, processing, marketing, and the cost of stripping the overlying waste material.¹⁶ The UPL aims to achieve the highest possible undiscounted cash flow, although modern approaches often prioritize Net Present Value (NPV).¹⁶
  • Resource Conservation: Beyond immediate profitability, the UPL also considers the conservation of resources, ensuring that any block meeting the economic criteria is included in the pit.¹⁶
  • Dynamic Nature: The ultimate pit limit is not a static boundary but a function of financial conditions and other determinative factors that can fluctuate over time, such as commodity prices and operating costs.¹⁶ This necessitates periodic re-evaluation and potential adjustment of the UPL.
  • Optimization Algorithms: Various algorithms are employed to determine the UPL. One popular approach is the Lerchs-Grossmann algorithm, which operates by assigning economic values (positive for ore, negative for waste) to individual blocks within a block model. The algorithm then identifies the largest collection of blocks that, when extracted, yield the maximum total economic value while adhering to slope stability constraints.¹⁶ This algorithm can generate a series of nested pits, each optimal for different economic conditions, allowing for production planning under varying market scenarios.¹⁶
  • Positional Weight: Some heuristic approaches, like that utilized by Wang and Sevim, derive a "positional weight" for each block. This weight considers the quality of the ore, the block's position, and the quality of ore beneath it, providing a measure of the desirability of removing a given block at a specific time.¹⁶

The ultimate pit limit is distinct from intermediate pits, which are configurations of the mine at various stages of development between its inception and final closure.¹⁶ The primary goal of UPL optimization is to ensure that the final pit design maximizes the economic return from the mineral deposit, taking into account the intricate interdependencies between ore and waste regions and the financial implications of extraction.

2.1. Derivation of Formula for Horizontal and Vertical Distances Determined by Stadia Tacheometry with the Condition of Staff Held Vertical

Tacheometry is a branch of surveying where horizontal and vertical distances are determined by optical means, primarily using angular observations with an instrument called a tacheometer. This method is particularly useful in rough or difficult terrain where direct linear measurements with chain or tape are impractical or tedious.¹⁸ The stadia method is the most widely used tacheometric procedure, based on the principle of similar isosceles triangles.¹⁷

When the staff is held vertically and the line of sight is inclined, the horizontal distance (H) and vertical distance (V) can be derived using the following formulas:

Horizontal Distance (H):

The horizontal distance from the instrument station to the staff station, when the line of sight is inclined at a vertical angle $\theta$ and the staff is held vertically, is given by:

$$H=Kscos^2\theta+Ccos\theta¹⁹$$

Where:

  • K is the multiplying constant or stadia interval factor, defined as f/i (focal length of the objective divided by the interval between stadia hairs).¹⁷ For many tacheometers, K is designed to be 100.¹⁷
  • s is the staff intercept, which is the difference between the readings of the upper and lower stadia hairs on the staff (e.g., $S=$ Upper reading - Lower reading).¹⁷
  • $\theta$ is the vertical angle of the line of sight (angle of elevation or depression) read on the vertical circle of the instrument.¹⁹
  • C is the additive constant of the tacheometer, defined as (f+d) (focal length of the objective plus the distance from the optical center of the objective to the vertical axis of the instrument).¹⁷ Modern tacheometers are often fitted with an anallatic lens to make the additive constant C zero, simplifying computations.¹⁷

Vertical Distance (V):

The vertical distance between the horizontal axis of the telescope and the middle cross-hair reading on the staff is given by:

$$V=Kssin\theta cos\theta+Csin\theta¹⁹$$

This can also be written as:

$$V=\frac{1}{2}Kssin~2\theta+C~sin~\theta¹⁹$$

The difference in elevation between the instrument station (P) and the staff station (Q) can then be calculated as:

$$RL~of~Q=RL~of~P+Height~of~Instrument~(h)+V-Middle~Hair~Reading~(r)^{20}$$

Determination of Tacheometric Constants (K and C):

The constants K and C for a specific tacheometer can be determined in the field:

  1. A line of approximately 100 meters is measured on fairly level ground, and pegs are driven at regular intervals (e.g., 50 meters).¹⁷
  2. The staff is held vertically at these predetermined stations, and corresponding staff intercepts (s) are observed with horizontal sight (i.e., $\theta=0$).¹⁷
  3. For horizontal sight, the distance formula simplifies to $D=Ks+C$ (since $cos^2\theta =1$ and $cos\theta=1$). By knowing the measured distances (D) and observed staff intercepts (s) for at least two points, a system of simultaneous equations can be formed. Solving these equations for successive pairs of observations yields values for K and C, and their average can be taken as the instrument's constants. For example, if $D1=Ks1+C$ and $D2=Ks2+C$, these can be solved to find K and C.¹⁹

2.2. Describe Prismatic Compass with Neat Sketch

►

A prismatic compass is a portable and specialized surveying instrument used primarily to measure the magnetic bearings of lines and angles between two points.²¹ It utilizes the Earth's magnetic field to determine directions and is widely applied in navigation and surveying tasks requiring angular measurements.²¹ Its name is derived from the presence of a prismatic lens, which facilitates accurate reading of the graduated circle simultaneously with sighting the object.²¹

Components of a Prismatic Compass:

A typical prismatic compass consists of the following essential parts:²¹

  1. Cylindrical Metal Box: This is the main body of the compass, usually made of non-magnetic metal (e.g., brass or aluminum), which houses and protects all internal components from external damage and ensures durability.²¹
  2. Pivot: A small, pointed, hardened steel support fixed at the center of the compass box. The magnetic needle is balanced on this pivot, allowing it to rotate freely and align itself with the Earth's magnetic meridian.²¹
  3. Magnetic Needle: A broad, highly magnetized needle that is balanced on the pivot. It aligns itself with the Earth's magnetic field, pointing towards magnetic north, and is crucial for determining bearings.²¹
  4. Graduated Circle (Ring): A circular ring-shaped disc, typically made of aluminum, attached to the magnetic needle. It is graduated in degrees from $0^{\circ}$ to $360^{\circ}$ in a clockwise direction. The readings are inverted on the ring so that they appear erect when viewed through the prism.²¹
  5. Prism: A small triangular prism positioned near the graduated circle. It magnifies the readings on the graduated circle and allows the surveyor to read the bearing while simultaneously sighting the object, ensuring accuracy and clear visibility of measurements.²¹ It often includes a focusing stud for clear visibility.²²
  6. Object Vane (Sighting Vane): A vertical vane with a fine wire or hairline, located opposite the prism. It is used for sighting the object or landmark being surveyed, helping to align the compass precisely with the target line.²¹
  7. Eye Vane (Eye Slit): A vertical slit or eye hole located on the opposite side of the object vane, through which the surveyor observes the target and reads the bearing.²¹ It ensures proper alignment of the sighting line.²¹
  8. Lifting Pin and Lever: A mechanism that lifts the magnetic needle off the pivot when the compass is not in use or when the sighting line is adjusted. This prevents unnecessary wear and tear on the pivot and the needle.²¹
  9. Spring Brake (Braking Pin): A small brass knob or lever that, when pressed, quickly dampens the oscillations of the magnetic needle, allowing for stable and quicker readings.²¹
  10. Glass Cover: A transparent glass cover protects the graduated circle and magnetic needle from dust, moisture, and other external elements.²¹
  11. Sunglasses (Colored Glasses): Small tinted glasses attached to the eye vane, used to reduce glare and provide clear visibility when sighting objects in bright sunlight.²¹
  12. Reflector Mirror: Sometimes included on the object vane to help observe objects located at higher or lower elevations without changing the instrument's position.²¹

Working Principle:

The working principle of a prismatic compass is based on traversing, where a network of interconnected survey lines is created by measuring distances and magnetic bearings.²²

  1. The compass is set up at a station, typically on a tripod or stand, and leveled.
  2. The surveyor sights the target object through the eye slit and the object vane, aligning the hairline with the target.
  3. As the sighting line is aligned, the magnetic needle, which is attached to the graduated circle, freely rotates and aligns itself with the magnetic meridian.
  4. The surveyor simultaneously looks through the prism to read the bearing directly from the graduated circle. The prism magnifies the inverted graduations, making them appear erect and allowing for precise readings without needing to shift the eye.²¹
  5. The spring brake can be used to quickly bring the oscillating needle to rest for a stable reading.²¹
  6. The reading obtained is the Whole Circle Bearing (WCB) of the line, measured clockwise from magnetic north.²²

A key advantage of the prismatic compass is that it simplifies the process of measuring bearings without requiring triangulation, unlike some other surveying methods.²² This makes it a compact and portable instrument suitable for various field surveys requiring angular measurements.²²

3. Question 3: Explain the whole process of preparation of EMP for an upcoming new open-cast project of coal deposit.

►

The preparation of an Environmental Management Plan (EMP) for an upcoming new open-cast coal mining project is a comprehensive and iterative process designed to identify, assess, mitigate, and monitor potential environmental impacts throughout the project's lifecycle, from construction and operation to decommissioning and post-closure rehabilitation.²³ The overarching goal is to minimize operational impacts, restore disturbed areas, ensure site safety, and control erosion and pollution, often guided by principles like Best Available Technology Not Exceeding Excessive Cost (BATNEEC).²³

The whole process typically involves several interconnected stages:

3.1. Initial Environmental Investigations and Scoping

Before an EMP can be drafted, extensive environmental investigations are conducted. This includes preparing an Environmental Scoping Report (ESR), which identifies all potential environmental impacts of the proposed mining and associated activities across various phases.²³ This initial assessment forms the foundation upon which the EMP is built, highlighting areas that require specific management measures.²³ For an open-cast coal project, this involves analyzing the proposed excavation of land, removal of overburden, and creation of external dumps, as well as land requirements for infrastructure like coal handling plants (CHPs), stockyards, and roads.²⁴

3.2. Formulation of Environmental Objectives

Based on the initial assessments, specific environmental objectives are established for various aspects of the project. These objectives guide the development of mitigation and management strategies.²³ Examples of such objectives include:

  • Topography: Mitigate impacts during operation and restore the topography as close as possible to its original form.²³
  • Geology: Limit impact on the mining area by restricting disturbed size and ensuring proper stockpiling of overburden and soils.²³
  • Soils: Mitigate long-term contamination, maintain viability for rehabilitation, ameliorate altered properties, install erosion control, monitor dust, and prevent contamination.²³
  • Land Capability & Use: Reduce disturbed area, contain impacts from equipment, and restore affected surface to agricultural use (e.g., grazing).²³
  • Biodiversity: Reduce impact on natural biodiversity, control alien plant invasion, encourage vegetation for erosion control, and ensure rehabilitation for habitat restoration.²³
  • Hydrogeology: Ensure effective water management of the site, including surface and groundwater quality and quantity.²³
  • Noise: Ensure blasting complies with safety acts, is controlled and monitored, and reduce mining noise impact.²³
  • Visual: Limit visual impact on road-users and local residents.²³
  • Socio-economic: Limit negative impacts from cessation of mining activities.²³
  • Maintenance: Monitor and manage post-closure impacts until closure is obtained.²³
  • Infrastructure: Ensure proper removal and disposal of components within the rehabilitation plan.²³
  • Waste: Collect and dispose of all waste at a permitted disposal site.²³

3.3. Development of Management Measures for Specific Activities

This is the core of the EMP, detailing specific mitigation and management measures for each mining activity across its various phases (construction, operation, decommissioning, closure). These measures are typically outlined in tabular format, specifying actions, frequency of implementation, and financial provisions.²³ Examples include:

  • Access and On-site Haul Roads: Measures include maintaining logs of soil stripping and placement, separate stockpiling of soil types, implementing waste management plans for chemical spills (oils, grease, diesel), containing surface water runoff, designing road drainage to dissipate flow, reinforcing roadsides, implementing dust suppression techniques (speed limits, wet suppression), and ensuring regular vehicle maintenance for noise reduction and safety.²³
  • Office and Plant Complexes: Focus on proper soil stripping and stockpiling, waste management for chemical spills and domestic/industrial waste, maintaining clean and dirty water infrastructures, minimizing stripped areas for dust control, and using noise reducers on machinery. 4. Question 4: Influence of Joints on Slope Stability and Stereographic Projection

►

4.1. Explain the Influence of Joints on Slope Stability

Geological joints, along with other discontinuities such as fractures, faults, and bedding planes, exert a profound influence on the stability and overall behavior of rock masses.²⁵ These discontinuities represent the weakest components within a rock mass and are the primary controlling factors for the strength and stability of both natural and engineered rock slopes.²⁵

The impact of joints on slope stability can be understood through several key aspects:

  • Reduced Rock Mass Strength: Intact rock material typically possesses high strength. However, the presence of joints significantly reduces the overall strength of the rock mass. Failure in a rock slope often occurs along these pre-existing planes of weakness rather than through the intact rock material.²⁵
  • Failure Modes: The geometry and mechanical properties of joints dictate the potential failure modes in rock slopes:
    • Planar Failure: Occurs when a single, persistent joint plane dips out of the slope at an angle greater than its friction angle, and the driving force (weight of the rock block) exceeds the joint's shearing strength.²⁵
    • Wedge Failure: Involves the failure of a rock mass along the intersection of two or more joint planes that dip out of the slope.
    • Toppling Failure: Occurs when steep, near-vertical joints dip into the slope, causing rock columns to rotate and fall forward.
  • Joint Parameters and Their Influence:
    • Dip Angle: The angle at which a joint plane is inclined relative to the horizontal is a critical factor. Shallow joint dip angles can lead to tensile failures, where the rock pulls apart due to tensile stresses. Conversely, steeper joint dip angles often result in shear-tensile failures, a combination of shearing along the joint and tensile fracturing of the rock.²⁵
    • Spacing: The distance between adjacent joints. Wider joint spacings generally lead to deeper failure zones and a higher factor of safety, as there is more intact rock to resist failure.²⁵
    • Orientation (Strike and Dip Direction): The strike and dip direction of joints relative to the slope face are paramount. Joints dipping parallel or sub-parallel to the slope face are most critical for stability.
    • Continuity/Persistence: This refers to the extent or length of a joint plane. Fully persistent joints, which extend across the entire rock mass, create continuous planes of weakness, leading to larger and shallower mobilized zones of failure compared to slopes with non-persistent joints.²⁵ Non-persistent joints, which are interrupted by intact rock bridges, can significantly enhance slope stability. The intact rock bridges act as "locked sections," requiring additional energy to fracture before a complete failure surface can develop.²⁵ Slopes with non-persistent discontinuities generally exhibit a higher factor of safety and significantly smaller mobilized zones.²⁵
    • Roughness (Joint Roughness Coefficient - JRC): The irregularities on the joint surface affect its shear strength. A higher JRC indicates greater interlocking and thus higher shear resistance along the joint.
    • Cohesion and Friction Angle: These are inherent shear strength parameters of the joint surface. Higher cohesion and friction angles contribute to greater stability.

The inherent uncertainty in characterizing rock joints imposes limitations on the reliability of predictions regarding rock mass behavior.²⁵ Therefore, comprehensive analysis that quantifies the impact of both persistent and non-persistent joints with various parameters is essential for sustainable rock engineering practices and the design of resilient structures in natural environments.²⁵ This understanding allows engineers to apply appropriate stabilization measures, such as rock bolting, shotcreting, or drainage, to ensure the long-term stability of rock slopes.

4.2. Explain the Procedure for Construction of Great Circle by Using the Stereographic Projection with Required Sketches.

►

Stereographic projection is a fundamental graphical technique in structural geology and rock mechanics used to represent three-dimensional geological features, such as planes and lines, on a two-dimensional plane.²⁷ A "great circle" in stereographic projection represents a plane, specifically the trace formed by the intersection of a plane passing through the center of a sphere with the sphere's surface.²⁷ Geologists typically use the lower hemisphere for projections.²⁷

The procedure for constructing a great circle using a stereonet (a pre-made grid of great and small circles) involves several steps:

Required Materials:

  • Stereonet: A transparent or translucent circular grid with a family of great circles intersecting at the top and bottom points, and small circles.²⁸ Every fifth cyclographic trace is often bolder for easier counting.²⁸
  • Tracing Paper: A sheet of tracing paper, cut to the same radius as the stereonet.²⁸
  • Pencil and Eraser: For drawing and marking.
  • Thumb Tack or Pin: To secure the tracing paper to the center of the stereonet.²⁷

Procedure for Constructing a Great Circle (representing a plane):

  1. Prepare the Overlay:
    • Place the tracing paper over the stereonet, aligning their centers. Secure them with a thumb tack or pin at the center.²⁷
    • Draw a circle on the tracing paper, tracing the primitive circle (the outermost circle) of the stereonet. This circle is also known as the primitive.²⁷
    • Mark the center of this circle with a cross.
    • Mark the cardinal directions (North, East, South, West or 090, 180, 270) on the primitive at $90^{\circ}$ intervals, with North at the top.²⁷ This establishes the reference orientation.
  2. Orient the Strike:
    • Rotate the tracing paper (overlay) around the central pin until the strike direction of the plane (e.g., N30E or 030°) aligns with the North-South line on the stereonet. For a plane with a strike of N30E, rotate the tracing paper so that the $030^{\circ}$ mark on the primitive (which you would have marked or visualized) aligns with the North point of the underlying stereonet.²⁷
  3. Plot the Dip and Trace the Great Circle:
    • From the primitive circle, along the East-West line (which is perpendicular to the strike direction on the stereonet), count inwards the number of degrees corresponding to the dip angle of the plane.²⁷ For example, if the plane dips $30^{\circ}$ to the SE, you would count $30^{\circ}$ inwards from the primitive along the East line (towards the center).²⁷
    • Identify the great circle on the stereonet that passes through this marked dip point and intersects the primitive at the strike orientation.²⁷
    • Carefully trace this great circle onto your tracing paper.²⁷ This traced curve represents the stereographic projection of the plane.
  4. Return to Reference Position:
    • Rotate the tracing paper back to its original orientation, with its North mark aligned with the North of the stereonet.²⁷ The traced great circle now correctly represents the orientation of the plane.

Interpretation of Great Circles:

  • Curvature: Planes that dip at low angles are represented by great circles with significant curvature, lying closer to the primitive circle. A horizontal plane (0° dip) is represented by the primitive circle itself.²⁷
  • Proximity to Center: Steeply dipping planes are characterized by great circles with minimal curvature, passing closer to the center of the plot. A vertical plane ($90^{\circ}$ dip) is represented by a straight line passing through the center of the stereonet.²⁸
  • Pole to a Plane: For every great circle, there is a corresponding "pole," which is a point representing the line perpendicular to the plane. The pole is plotted $90^{\circ}$ away from the great circle along the same radius.²⁷ If the great circle is near the primitive, its pole will be near the center, and vice versa.

Stereographic projection is a powerful tool for analyzing structural data, such as finding the intersection of two planes, the angle between two lines, or fitting a plane to multiple lines.²⁷

5. Question 5: Mineral Inventory, Ore Reserve & Grade Tonnage Curves

5.1. Define the terms 'mineral inventory' and 'ore reserve'.

In the context of mining and resource estimation, the terms 'mineral inventory' and 'ore reserve' are distinct and represent different stages of economic viability and confidence in a mineral deposit.

Mineral Inventory (Mineral Resource):

The mineral inventory, more formally known as a mineral resource, refers to a concentration or occurrence of material of intrinsic economic interest in or on the Earth's crust in such form, quality, and quantity that there are reasonable prospects for eventual economic extraction.²⁹ It includes mineralization for which location, grade, quantity, geological characteristics, and mineral content are known, estimated, or interpreted from specific geological evidence and knowledge.²⁹

The mineral inventory encompasses all identified mineralization, regardless of its current economic profitability.³⁰ It is a broader category that includes material that may not be profitable to mine under current economic conditions or with current technology, but which might become so in the future. Mineral resources are often classified into categories such as Inferred, Indicated, and Measured, based on increasing levels of geological confidence and data density.²⁹

Ore Reserve (Mineral Reserve):

An ore reserve, or mineral reserve, is the economically mineable part of a Measured and/or Indicated Mineral Resource.²⁹ It includes diluting materials and allowances for losses that may occur when the material is mined. The key distinction is that an ore reserve implies that the material can be mined at a profit under prevailing or assumed economic conditions and with current mining and processing technology.³⁰

To classify a mineral resource as an ore reserve, various "modifying factors" must be considered and demonstrated to be viable. These factors include mining, processing, metallurgical, infrastructure, economic, marketing, legal, environmental, social, and governmental considerations.¹² Essentially, the ore reserve is that portion of the mineral inventory that has been demonstrated to be economically extractable after applying all relevant modifying factors. As the selling price of the mineral increases or the extraction costs decrease, a larger proportion of the mineral inventory can be classified as ore reserve.³⁰ Ore reserves are typically classified as Proved or Probable, reflecting different levels of confidence in their economic viability and geological certainty.²⁹

In summary, the mineral inventory represents the total geological occurrence of mineralization with reasonable prospects for future economic extraction, while the ore reserve is the subset of the mineral inventory that is currently demonstrated to be economically viable for extraction.

5.2. Explain about grade tonnage curves and its computations.

►

Grade-tonnage curves are fundamental tools in the mining industry, widely used across geology, mining planning, management, and investment for economic and financial analysis of mineral deposits.³² These curves graphically represent the relationship between the average grade of a mineral deposit and the cumulative tonnage of material available at or above a given cutoff grade.¹⁴ They illustrate how variations in the cutoff grade impact the quantity and quality of the recoverable resource.³²

Concept and Significance:

A grade-tonnage curve typically depicts an "optimistic" or "best-case" scenario, assuming that at any given cutoff grade, all material above that grade is ideally available for mining.³² This implies total continuity of mineralization and assumes every block is equally accessible for mining, irrespective of its location or relationship to neighboring blocks.³²

The curves are invaluable for:

  • Resource Evaluation: They help in understanding the characteristics of a deposit and how its tonnage and grade change with different economic thresholds (cutoff grades).¹⁴
  • Economic Analysis: They are crucial for economic and financial assessments, allowing stakeholders to evaluate the sensitivity of the resource to changes in commodity prices and mining costs.¹⁴ For instance, if commodity prices rise, lower-grade material becomes economic, which can be visualized on the curve as an increase in recoverable tonnage at a lower cutoff grade.¹⁴
  • Mine Planning: While typically calculated from in-situ resources or reserves without direct consideration of mining method geometry or spatial continuity, they provide initial insights for strategic mine planning decisions.³²

Limitations:

A significant limitation of standard grade-tonnage curves is their lack of information regarding continuity.³² They are based on a block model that counts every single block above a cutoff grade, regardless of its isolation or impracticality of mining.³² In reality, geological characteristics and grade distribution can lead to complex geometries, meaning that not all blocks above a certain cutoff may be economically mineable due to lack of continuity or accessibility.³² The more variable the grades, the more complex the geometry, and the less reliable the curve becomes as a direct predictor of mineable tonnage.³²

Computations and Methods:

Grade-tonnage curves are primarily computed from geological block models of a mineral deposit. The general approach involves:

  1. Block Model Creation: The deposit is discretized into a grid of uniform blocks, and each block is assigned a grade based on exploration data (drill holes, samples) using geostatistical estimation techniques (e.g., kriging).³²
  2. Iterative Cutoff Application: The computation involves iterating through a range of increasing cutoff grades:
    • For each selected cutoff grade, all blocks in the model with an estimated grade equal to or greater than that cutoff are identified.
    • The total tonnage of these selected blocks is summed.
    • The average grade of these selected blocks is calculated.
  3. Data Plotting: The cumulative tonnage and corresponding average grade for each cutoff grade are then plotted on a graph, with cutoff grade typically on the x-axis and cumulative tonnage and/or average grade on the y-axis.

Mathematical Models (Lasky's Equation):

Historically, empirical relationships have been proposed to describe grade-tonnage relationships. One well-known example is Lasky's equation (1950), which suggests a linear relationship between the average grade (x) and the logarithm of the cumulative ore tonnage (T) above a given grade x.³³ The equation is typically expressed as:

$$\log T(x)=a-bx$$

where a and b are positive constants, and $T(x)$ is the cumulative ore tonnage above grade x.³³ This equation implies that as the grade decreases, the cumulative tonnage increases logarithmically. However, it has limitations, particularly in the low-grade part where economic factors might lead to a flattening of the curve, as low-grade deposits may not be reported or become economically viable.³³

More advanced computational methods may involve:

  • Simulated Images: Generating multiple simulated images of the orebody to provide a grade-tonnage curve along with an uncertainty range for the unconstrained situation.³²
  • Continuity Factor: In a second step, a continuity factor derived from the specific mining method can be applied to constrain the blocks. This involves computing continuous sets of blocks above each cutoff grade to yield a "corrected" grade-tonnage curve that better reflects mineable reserves.³² An uncertainty range can also be derived from these simulated images.³²

While the grade-tonnage curve provides a valuable initial overview of a deposit's potential, its approximation to reality depends significantly on the geology and grade distribution. Geologists and mining engineers, with their expert understanding of mineralization, are best equipped to interpret these curves and account for continuity issues that might not be explicitly captured in the basic computation.³²

6. Question 6: Explain with neat and labelled sketch, the arrangement and working of a diamond wire saw suitable for marble block production.

A diamond wire saw is a cutting-edge technology used in stone mines, particularly for the precise and efficient production of marble blocks. It offers significant advantages over traditional methods like gangsaws, including higher material utilization, improved slab integrity, and greater processing capacity.³⁴

Arrangement of a Diamond Wire Saw:

The diamond wire saw system primarily consists of the following structural components:

  1. Diamond Wire: This is the core cutting tool. It is composed of a high-strength steel wire rope, which serves as the main load-bearing element. Along the length of this steel rope, diamond beads are firmly attached, typically separated by springs. The diamond beads are responsible for the actual cutting action, while the springs provide shock absorption and maintain consistent cutting gaps.³⁵ The diameter of these diamond wires can be as low as 0.35 mm, significantly reducing material loss (kerf) compared to traditional saw blades.³⁴
  2. Drive System: This system provides the power to rotate the diamond wire at high speeds (typically 20-40 m/s for marble). It usually comprises an electric or hydraulic motor, a gearbox to transmit power, and large drive wheels (or flywheels) around which the diamond wire is wrapped.³⁴
  3. Guidance System: This system ensures the diamond wire maintains stability and follows the correct trajectory during cutting. It consists of multiple guide wheels (or pulleys) and supporting structures. These wheels direct the wire into the stone block at the desired angle and position.³⁵
  4. Tensioning System: Crucial for efficient and effective cutting, the tensioning system adjusts and maintains optimal tension in the diamond wire. Proper tension ensures consistent contact with the stone, prevents slack, and maximizes cutting efficiency.³⁵
  5. Cooling System: Diamond wire cutting generates significant heat and dust. The cooling system uses a continuous flow of water to cool both the diamond wire and the cutting surface. This prevents overheating damage to the tool and the stone, while also flushing away debris and dust generated during the cutting process.³⁵

Working of a Diamond Wire Saw for Marble Block Production:

The operational steps for using a diamond wire saw in marble quarries are as follows:

  1. Site Survey and Preparation:
    • Before any cutting commences, a detailed geological survey of the marble deposit is conducted. This involves assessing the type, hardness, texture direction, and structural features (e.g., joints, faults) of the stone.³⁵
    • Based on this assessment and the desired block dimensions, a precise cutting plan is developed. This plan specifies the exact cutting positions, directions, depths, and the sequence of cuts to optimize block recovery and minimize waste.³⁵
    • Necessary equipment, including the diamond wire saw machine, power generator, a reliable source of cooling water, and anchoring equipment (for securing the machine and guide wheels), are prepared and brought to the site.³⁵
  2. Equipment Installation and Commissioning:
    • The diamond wire saw machine is installed at the selected position according to the cutting plan. This involves securely anchoring the main frame of the machine.
    • The guide wheels and drive wheels are then installed, ensuring their precise position and angle meet the design requirements for the planned cuts.³⁵
    • The diamond wire is carefully threaded through the guide wheels and wrapped around the drive wheels, forming a continuous loop. The ends of the wire are then connected to the tensioning system.³⁵
    • The drive system is started slowly, and the tensioning system is adjusted to apply the appropriate tightness to the diamond wire, ensuring stability and preventing slippage during cutting.³⁵
  3. Cutting Operation:
    • Once the system is set up and tensioned, the drive motor rotates the drive wheels, causing the diamond wire to move at high speed.
    • The diamond beads embedded in the wire come into contact with the marble block. Through continuous friction, impact, and abrasion, the diamond beads grind and cut through the stone.³⁵ The high speed and the hardness of the diamonds enable efficient material removal.
    • Simultaneously, the cooling system continuously supplies water to the cutting zone. This water cools the wire and the stone, preventing thermal damage, and also flushes away the stone slurry (a mixture of rock particles and water) from the cutting kerf, keeping the cutting path clear.³⁵
    • The machine typically moves along a track or rail, or the guide wheels are adjusted, to advance the cut through the rock mass. Multiple parallel wires can be used simultaneously in multi-wire saws to produce several slabs from a single block.³⁴
    • The cutting process continues until the desired block is liberated from the quarry face.

Advantages for Marble Block Production:

  • High Material Utilization: Diamond wire saws create a very narrow cutting kerf (as low as 0.35 mm), significantly reducing material loss compared to traditional gangsaws (1.8 mm blade thickness), which is particularly critical for valuable luxury stones like marble.³⁴ This can lead to a 10-25% material saving.³⁴
  • Superior Slab Integrity: The continuous, tension-controlled cutting action of diamond wire saws minimizes structural damage and micro-cracks in fragile marble, which can be induced by the reciprocating motion of gangsaws.³⁴ This results in higher quality slabs with better surface finish, often ready for direct polishing.³⁴
  • Increased Processing Capacity: Multi-wire saws can handle large blocks (up to 3 meters in height) and employ numerous wires (up to 120 simultaneously), significantly increasing production output.³⁴
  • Environmental Benefits: The cutting process minimizes impact forces, reducing breakage and adhesive requirements, which can lead to lower VOC emissions in marble fabrication.³⁴ They also offer energy reduction and less noise compared to traditional methods.³⁴

    • If an Inspector has reason to believe, based on an inspection, examination, or inquiry, that an offense under the Act has been or is being committed, they may search any place and take possession of any material, plan, section, register, or other record relevant to the offense.³⁸ This power is crucial for gathering evidence for legal proceedings.
  6. Issuing Notices for Dangerous Conditions (Section 22(1)):
    • If an Inspector determines that any mine or part thereof, or any matter, thing, or practice connected with the mine, is dangerous to human life or safety, or is defective in a way that threatens bodily injury, and for which no express provision is made in the Act, they may issue a written notice to the owner, agent, or manager.³⁹ The notice must specify the particulars of the danger or defect and require it to be remedied within a specified time and manner.³⁹
  7. Prohibiting Employment (Section 22(1A)):
    • If the owner, agent, or manager fails to comply with a notice issued under Section 22(1) within the specified period, the Chief Inspector or Inspector may, by written order, prohibit the employment of any person in or about the mine or any part thereof, if their employment is not deemed reasonably necessary for securing compliance with the notice.³⁹ This provides a direct means to prevent exposure to immediate hazards.
  8. Prohibiting Extraction or Reduction of Pillars (Section 22(2)):
    • Without prejudice to other provisions, the Chief Inspector or Inspector can issue a written order to prohibit the extraction or reduction of pillars or blocks of minerals in any mine or part thereof.³⁹ This power is invoked if, in their opinion, such operations are likely to cause crushing of pillars, premature collapse of workings, or otherwise endanger the mine or the life and safety of persons employed therein. This also applies if adequate provision against fire or flooding has not been made.³⁹ This power is vital for preventing catastrophic ground control failures and major incidents.

These powers collectively enable the Inspectors of Mines to effectively enforce safety regulations, investigate incidents, and take immediate corrective actions to safeguard lives and property within the mining industry.

8.1. What are the salient features mentioned under UNFC method of computing reserve estimation?

The United Nations Framework Classification for Resources (UNFC) is a comprehensive, three-dimensional, code-based system designed for the classification and reporting of mineral resources.²⁹ It provides a standardized framework that facilitates the comparison of different coal and mineral commodities and resolves semantic problems that previously existed in resource classification.²⁹ The UNFC system is characterized by its three axes, each representing a key aspect of resource assessment:

  1. Economic Viability (E-axis): This axis represents the socio-economic viability of the project and is the first digit in the UNFC code.²⁹ It has three categories, decreasing in order of viability:
    • E1 (Economic): Resources that are currently economically viable for extraction.²⁹
    • E2 (Potentially Economic): Resources that are not currently economic but are expected to become so in the foreseeable future.²⁹
    • E3 (Intrinsically Economic): Resources that are not currently economic and whose economic viability is not yet established or is intrinsically low.²⁹
  2. Feasibility Assessment (F-axis): This axis represents the status of feasibility studies and is the second digit in the UNFC code.²⁹ It also has three categories, decreasing in order of assessment level:
    • F1 (Feasibility Study and Mining Report): Projects for which a detailed feasibility study and mining report have been completed, demonstrating technical and economic viability.²⁹
    • F2 (Pre-feasibility Study): Projects for which a pre-feasibility study has been completed, indicating potential viability but requiring further detailed assessment.²⁹
    • F3 (Geological Study): Projects where only geological studies have been conducted, without detailed engineering or economic evaluations.²⁹
  3. Geological Assessment (G-axis): This axis represents the level of geological knowledge and confidence in the resource and is the third digit in the UNFC code.²⁹ The geological assessment process typically involves stages of increasing detail, generating data with clearly defined degrees of geological assurance. It has four categories:
    • G1 (Detailed Exploration): Resources estimated with a high level of confidence based on detailed exploration, sampling, and testing (e.g., proved reserves).²⁹
    • G2 (General Exploration): Resources estimated with a reasonable level of confidence based on general exploration, where boreholes or pits may be more widely spaced (e.g., indicated resources).²⁹
    • G3 (Prospecting): Estimates based on prospecting activities, involving preliminary field inspections and geological inference.²⁹
    • G4 (Reconnaissance): Estimates based on regional geological studies, mapping, and indirect methods.²⁹

Combined Classification:

The UNFC uses a three-digit code (E-F-G) to classify resources. The highest category of resources under the UNFC system, representing the highest confidence and economic viability, is coded as (111).²⁹ This typically corresponds to "Proved Mineral Reserve" - an economically mineable part of a Measured and/or Indicated Mineral Resource.²⁹ The lowest category is (334).⁴⁰

Other Salient Features:

  • Flexibility: UNFC is not a rigid system, allowing for adaptability to various mineral commodities and geological settings.²⁹
  • Clarity and Unambiguity: It provides short, unambiguous identification of reserve/resource classes, which facilitates computer processing of data and information exchange.²⁹
  • Total Mineral Resource: The total mineral resource is defined as the sum of reserves and additional or remaining resources.²⁹
  • Uneconomic Occurrence: Materials that are too low in grade or for other reasons are not considered potentially economic are classified as "Uneconomic Occurrence" and are not part of a Mineral Resource.²⁹
  • Commonly Used Categories: While 36 classifications are theoretically possible, only about 8 are frequently used in practice.²⁹

The UNFC provides a robust and internationally recognized framework for classifying mineral and energy resources, enabling consistent reporting and comparability across different regions and commodities.

8.2. What do you understand by 'reduction ratio'? Specify its importance on processing efficiency.

►

The 'reduction ratio' (often abbreviated as R/R) in mineral processing, particularly in crushing operations, is broadly defined as the ratio of the feed size to the product size.⁴¹ It is a crucial parameter for understanding the extent of size reduction achieved by a crusher and for selecting appropriate crushing equipment for a given application.⁴¹

Understanding Reduction Ratio:

No single crusher can produce a very fine product from an extremely coarse feed in a single pass. The reduction ratio quantifies this capability. There are various types of reduction ratios depending on the specific application and desired level of technical detail:

  • Limiting Reduction Ratio: This is the most commonly understood definition, representing the ratio of the maximum feed size (F100, i.e., the aperture through which 100% of the feed material passes) to the maximum product size (P100, i.e., the aperture through which 100% of the product material passes).⁴¹ While easy to estimate, it can be inaccurate due to the presence of a small proportion of coarse, slabby material that may not easily pass through screens.⁴¹
  • 80% Reduction Ratio (F80/P80): This is widely referred to by manufacturers and is considered a more reliable measure for sizing crushers or assessing performance. It is the ratio of the theoretical square mesh aperture that will pass 80% of the feed (F80) to the aperture that will pass 80% of the product (P80).⁴¹ This ratio mitigates inaccuracies caused by extreme particle sizes and is generally preferred for practical applications.⁴¹

For maximum jaw crusher efficiency, a target reduction ratio of 6-to-1 or 8-to-1 is often recommended. This range typically provides an ideal P80 factor, meaning 80% of the crushed material will pass screening after primary crushing.⁴²

Importance on Processing Efficiency:

The reduction ratio has significant importance on overall mineral processing efficiency:

  1. Crusher Sizing and Selection: The reduction ratio is a primary consideration when selecting a suitable crushing machine for a specific application.⁴¹ Manufacturers use this ratio to ensure the chosen unit can meet the customer's requirements for size reduction. An incorrectly sized crusher (e.g., one attempting too high a reduction ratio in a single stage) can lead to inefficiencies, excessive wear, and operational problems.
  2. Optimizing Crusher Performance: Fine-tuning the reduction ratio is critical for optimizing the performance and workflow of a crushing circuit.⁴² For instance, a smaller closed-side setting in a jaw crusher will produce more fines, affecting the product size distribution. The nip angle (angle at which material is gripped and crushed) is also influenced by settings and is crucial for proper crushing.⁴²
  3. Energy Consumption: Achieving the desired size reduction efficiently directly impacts energy consumption. An optimized reduction ratio ensures that the crusher operates within its design parameters, minimizing wasted energy and maximizing throughput.
  4. Downstream Process Efficiency: The product size distribution from crushing directly affects the efficiency of subsequent processing stages, such as grinding, screening, and mineral separation. If the reduction ratio is not appropriate, it can lead to:
    • Over-crushing: Producing excessive fines, which can be detrimental to some separation processes (e.g., gravity concentration) and increase energy consumption in the crushing stage.
    • Under-crushing: Leaving too many coarse particles, which can overload downstream grinding mills or lead to inefficient separation if liberation has not been achieved.
  5. Contaminant Separation: An optimized reduction ratio can facilitate easier separation of contaminants from the valuable minerals.⁴² By achieving the right particle size, the valuable minerals can be liberated from the gangue, making subsequent physical separation methods more effective.¹⁰
  6. Capacity and Throughput: The reduction ratio, along with other factors like feed rate and crusher size, directly influences the capacity of the crushing plant (e.g., short tons per hour).⁴² Maintaining a consistent feed rate is important, as frequent changes can reduce capacity and efficiency and cause issues downstream.⁴²

In essence, the reduction ratio is not merely a descriptive parameter but a critical operational variable that, when properly managed, ensures efficient size reduction, optimizes downstream mineral processing, and contributes significantly to the overall economic viability of a mining operation.
